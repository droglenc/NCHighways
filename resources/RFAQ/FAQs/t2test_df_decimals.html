The df for my two-sample t-test contains decimals.

There are two types of two-sample t-tests -- (1) variances are equal and (2) variances are unequal. In our introductory statistics course we only discussed the equal variances case. In the equal variances case the degrees-of-freedom are n1+n2-2. In the unequal variances case the degrees-of-freedom are computed from a more complicated formula that often results in fractions (decimals) of degrees-of-freedom. Thus, if you expected the two-sample t-test degrees-of-freedom to be n1+n2-2 then you forgot to set var.equal=TRUE in t.test(). Of course, before doing this you should verify with Levenes() that the variances are statistically equal

The following is the unequal variances two-sample t-test (note that the unequal variances version is also called the Welch two-sample t-test)

> t.test(extra ~ group)
        Welch Two Sample t-test
data:  extra by group 
t = -1.8608, df = 17.776, p-value = 0.0794
alternative hypothesis: true difference in means is not equal to 0 
95 percent confidence interval:
 -3.3654832  0.2054832 
sample estimates:
mean in group 1 mean in group 2 
           0.75            2.33

The following is the same test constructed as an equal variances two-sample t-test

> Levenes(extra~group)
Levene's Test for Homogeneity of Variance
      Df F value p-value
group  1  0.2482 0.62437
      18 
> t.test(extra ~ group,var.equal=TRUE)
        Two Sample t-test
data:  extra by group 
t = -1.8608, df = 18, p-value = 0.07919
alternative hypothesis: true difference in means is not equal to 0 
95 percent confidence interval:
 -3.3638740  0.2038740 
sample estimates:
mean in group 1 mean in group 2 
           0.75            2.33

Notice the difference in degrees-of-freedom between the two tests.
